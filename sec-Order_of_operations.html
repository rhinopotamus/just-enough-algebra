    Section 0.4 Prelude: Order of operations

    Remember Cole who was shocked by his credit card bill? His bill showed a previous balance of $529.16, credit for a payment of $200, finance charge of $42.78, a late fee of $30 (ouch!), a credit for $17.43 for a return he made, and $618.25 in new charges. How can we help Cole check his balance?
    üîó
    The most direct way to calculate Cole‚Äôs balance would be step-by-step.
    üîó
    \begin{align*} 529.16-200 \amp= \underline{329.16}\\ 329.16+42.78\amp=\underline{371.94}\\ 371.94+30\amp=\underline{401.94}\\ 401.94 -17.43\amp=\underline{384.51}\\ 384.51+618.25\amp=\underline{1002.76} \end{align*}
    The underlined numbers are what the calculator will show. Cole doesn‚Äôt type those numbers.
    üîó
    We can avoid typing in answers (which can lead to errors) by continuing one big calculation instead.
    üîó
    \begin{align*} 529.16-200 \amp=\underline{329.16}\\ +42.78\amp=\underline{371.94}\\ +30\amp= \underline{401.94}\\ -17.43\amp=\underline{384.51}\\ +618.25\amp=\underline{1002.76} \end{align*}
    Cole found an even quicker way to check the balance. He did not hit \(=\) until the very end of the calculation. He just did the calculation all in one line.
    \begin{equation*} 529.16 - 200 + 42.78 + 30 - 17.43 + 618.25 = \$1002.76 \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Notice that there are both addition (\(+\)) and subtraction (\(-\)) operations in this one-line calculation. These two operations (\(+\) and \(-\)) are tied for priority in the order of operations, so the calculator works from left to right. This one line calculation is equivalent to doing the much slower step-by-step process!
    üîó
    By the way, the word priority means of higher importance and, therefore, coming before. For example, doing your math homework should be a higher priority than watching random videos online. But getting a good night‚Äôs sleep should be a higher priority than studying for a test. (You‚Äôll have to trust me on that one.)
    üîó
    Earlier we heard about the Nussbaums who planted a walnut tree. The tree was 5 feet tall when the planted it. It has grown around 2 feet a year. According to this information, the tree was \(5+2=7\) feet tall after one year, \(7+2=9\) feet tall after two years, \(9+2=11\) feet tall after three years, and so on. We wanted to know how tall the tree was after 18 years.
    üîó
    One way to calculate the answer is to first figure out how much the tree grew in 18 years. That is
    \begin{equation*} \underbrace{2+2+ \ldots + 2}_\text{18 times} \end{equation*}
    which we calculate as
    \begin{equation*} 18 \times 2 = 36 \end{equation*}
    Next, we add the original height plus how much the tree grew to get
    \begin{equation*} 36+5=41 \end{equation*}
    The tree was 41 feet tall after 18 years.
    üîó
    Inspired by Cole‚Äôs one-line calculation, let‚Äôs do this calculation all in one line. One option is
    \begin{equation*} 18 \times 2 + 5= \end{equation*}
    Notice we are only hitting the \(=\) key at the end. We get 41 feet, again.
    üîó
    But wait! What if I wanted to enter the starting height first (5 feet) and then add on how much the tree grew (36 feet). I should be able to do that because \(5 + 36 = 41\) also. What happens when you enter
    \begin{equation*} 5+18 \times 2=? \end{equation*}
    You should get 41 again.
    üîó
    Something strange is happening. Either the calculator is reading your mind (probably not possible yet, but Artificial Intelligence is making great progress), or the calculator has a secret order of operations. The calculator is not just working from left to right because that would be
    üîó
    \begin{align*} 5+18\amp=\underline{23}\\ \times 2\amp=\underline{46}\text{ Oops!} \end{align*}
    That is not what the calculator did. The calculator first did the multiplication (\(18 \times 2 = 36\)) and then did the addition (\(5+36=41\)). It turns out that multiplication (\(\times\)) is higher priority than addition (\(+\)) and subtraction (\(-\)) in the order of operations.
    üîó
    You will need to know the full order of operations so that you know when the calculator is doing what you want. Here‚Äôs the full list of the order of operations, the priority ranking for arithmetic operations.
    üîó

    Order of operations

    • First, calculate anything inside Parentheses.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Next, calculate Exponents \(\wedge\text{,}\) in order from left to right.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Then, Multiply \(\times\) and Divide \(\div\text{,}\) in order from left to right.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Last, Add \(+\) and Subtract \(-\text{,}\) in order from left to right.
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    We highlighted the letters PEMDAS which often helps people remember this order. (‚ÄúPlease Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally‚Äù is how I learned it.) Outside of the United States this rule has names such as BEDMAS, BIDMAS, BODMAS, PODMAS, PIDMAS, or even GEMDAS. Notice that multiplication (\(\times\)) and division (\(\div\)) are tied for priority just like addition (\(+\)) and subtraction (\(-\)) were.
    üîó
    The good news is that your calculator does the operations in exactly this order. For example, remember Jarron who was calculating his BMI? He entered
    \begin{equation*} 91.625 \div 1.93 \wedge 2 = 24.7479\ldots \approx 24.8 \end{equation*}
    which is in the range considered healthy. Of course, there are many more factors to health besides height and weight. Like getting enough exercise is really important. But, back to the calculation, how did that work?
    üîó
    According to the order of operations, the calculator did the exponent (\(\wedge\)) first and then the division (\(\div\)) second. Let‚Äôs check it out:
    üîó
    \begin{align*} 1.93 \wedge 2 \amp=\underline{3.7249}\\ 91.625 \div 3.7249\amp=\underline{24.7479\ldots} \end{align*}
    That‚Äôs correct because exponents (\(\wedge\)) are higher priority in the order of operations than the other arithmetic operations (\(\times, \div, +, -\)).
    üîó
    Remember that \(1.93 \wedge 2\) is short for \(1.93\times 1.93\text{.}\) Don‚Äôt believe me? Try both on your calculator to see. Your calculator may have a key marked \(\wedge\) or it might be marked \(y^x\text{.}\) We will come back to exponents in more depth later.
    üîó
    What can we do if we want the calculator to work in a different order? For example, suppose in the Asian student association that there are 3 Chinese students, 15 Hmong students, 11 Vietnamese students, and 5 students who do not identify with any of these ethnicities. We‚Äôd like to report the percentage of students in the group who are Hmong.
    üîó
    First we need to calculate the total number of students
    \begin{equation*} 3+15+11+5 = 34 \end{equation*}
    There are 34 students and 15 identify as Hmong. That means the proportion of Hmong students is
    \begin{equation*} 15 \div 34 = 0.4411\ldots \end{equation*}
    and so the percentage of students in the group who are Hmong is
    \begin{equation*} 0.4411 \times 100 = 44.11 \approx 44 \% \end{equation*}
    Notice we can do these last two calculations in one line as
    \begin{equation*} 15 \div 34 \times 100 = 0.4411\ldots \end{equation*}
    because multiplication (\(\times\)) and division (\(\div\)) are tied for priority in the order of operations.
    üîó
    Can we do the entire calculation in one line? Sure, but it does not work to do
    \begin{equation*} 15 \div 3 + 15 + 11 + 5 \times 100=531 \text{ Oops!} \end{equation*}
    What did the calculator do? Since multiplication (\(\times\)) and division (\(\div\)) are higher priority than addition (\(+\)), the calculator first figured out that \(15\div 3 = 5\) and \(5 \times 100 = 500\) and then it calculated \(5 + 15 + 11 + 500 = 531\text{.}\) Argh.
    üîó
    We wanted the calculator to do the addition (\(+\)) first. That‚Äôs not the order of operations the calculator will use. What can we do to fix that? Parentheses to the rescue! Parentheses have the highest priority in the order of operations, meaning the calculator will evaluate inside the parentheses before doing any other operations. So we can do this calculation all in one line by using parentheses as
    \begin{equation*} 15 \div (3 + 15 + 11 + 5) \times 100= 44.11\ldots \text{ Whew!} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    As you work through the exercises, challenge yourself to find one-line calculations to use. You are always welcome to do the slower step-by-step method to check.
